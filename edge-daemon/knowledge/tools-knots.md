# Tools and Knots

## Essential Knots for Survival

Knowing how to tie reliable knots is fundamental to shelter building, trapping, climbing, first aid, and general camp craft. Learn these knots so well you can tie them in the dark with cold hands.

## The 10 Essential Knots

### 1. Bowline — The King of Knots
Creates a fixed loop that will not slip or tighten under load. Use for: anchoring shelters, rescue loops, securing loads.

**How to tie:** Form a small loop in the standing line (the "rabbit hole"). Pass the working end up through the loop (rabbit comes out of the hole), around behind the standing line (around the tree), and back down through the small loop (back into the hole). Tighten.

**Memory aid:** "The rabbit comes out of the hole, goes around the tree, and goes back down the hole."

### 2. Clove Hitch — Quick Attachment to a Post or Tree
Secures a rope to a cylindrical object. Quick to tie, easy to adjust. Use for: starting lashings, hanging bear bags, temporary attachments.

**How to tie:** Wrap rope around the post. Cross over the standing line and wrap around again. Tuck the working end under the last wrap. For security, add a half-hitch.

### 3. Taut-Line Hitch — Adjustable Tension
Creates a loop that slides to tighten but holds under load. Use for: tensioning guy lines, adjusting tarp shelters, clotheslines.

**How to tie:** Wrap the working end around the standing line twice on the inside of the loop. Make one more wrap on the outside. The knot slides freely when unloaded but grips when tension is applied.

### 4. Trucker's Hitch — Mechanical Advantage
Creates a simple pulley system for extreme tension. Use for: tightening ridgelines, securing heavy loads, creating very taut shelter lines.

**How to tie:** Create a fixed loop in the standing line (using an overhand-on-a-bight). Pass the working end around the anchor point and back through the loop. Pull to create 3:1 mechanical advantage. Secure with two half-hitches.

### 5. Square Knot (Reef Knot) — Binding
Joins two ropes of equal diameter. Use for: tying bandages, securing bundles, joining short ropes.

**How to tie:** Right over left and through, then left over right and through. Both working ends should exit on the same side of the knot. If they exit on opposite sides, you have a granny knot (insecure).

### 6. Sheet Bend — Joining Different Ropes
Joins two ropes of different diameters securely. Use for: extending ropes, joining cordage of different materials.

**How to tie:** Form a bight in the thicker rope. Pass the thinner rope up through the bight, around both parts of the bight, and tuck under itself. For extra security on slippery rope, make a double sheet bend (two passes around the bight).

### 7. Figure-8 Knot — Stopper
Prevents rope from pulling through a hole, pulley, or grommet. Use for: preventing rope end from fraying, climbing anchors, stopping lines from pulling through.

### 8. Prusik Knot — Climbing and Ascending
A friction hitch tied with a loop of thinner cord around a thicker rope. Grips when loaded, slides when unloaded. Use for: ascending a fixed rope, creating a safety catch, adjustable anchors.

### 9. Timber Hitch — Dragging Logs
Grips tighter under load. Self-releasing when slack. Use for: dragging logs, starting diagonal lashings, towing.

### 10. Two Half-Hitches — General Securing
Two half-hitches around a post or ring. Simple, reliable, and easy to untie. Use for: tying off boats, securing loads, general purpose attachment.

## Essential Lashings

### Square Lashing — 90-Degree Joints
Joins two poles at right angles. Used for building frames, shelters, tables, and rafts.

1. Start with a clove hitch on the vertical pole below the crossing point
2. Wrap the rope over the horizontal pole, behind the vertical, over the horizontal on the other side, and behind the vertical — creating a square pattern
3. Make 3-4 complete wrapping turns
4. Frapping turns: wrap tightly between the poles (perpendicular to the wrapping turns) 2-3 times to tighten the lashing
5. Finish with a clove hitch

### Diagonal Lashing — X-Shaped Joints
Joins two poles crossing diagonally. Prevents them from spreading apart.

### Shear Lashing — Parallel Poles
Joins two parallel poles for extending length or creating A-frames.

## Blade Sharpening

### Sharpening with a Stone
1. Start with a coarse grit stone (400 grit) if the blade is very dull or damaged
2. Lay the blade flat on the stone, then raise the spine to approximately 15-20 degrees (the thickness of two coins under the spine)
3. Maintain this angle consistently throughout all strokes
4. Push the blade forward across the stone as if trying to shave a thin layer off the stone surface
5. Apply moderate, even pressure
6. Count strokes — do equal numbers on each side
7. Progress to finer stone (1000+ grit) for a polished edge
8. Strop on leather strip (apply polishing compound if available) using trailing strokes
9. Test sharpness: the blade should shave arm hair or cleanly slice a piece of paper

### Field Sharpening Without a Stone
- Unglazed bottom ring of a ceramic mug works as an emergency sharpener
- Smooth river stones work but cut slowly
- Car window edge (top of rolled-down window) works for emergency touch-ups
- Leather belt as a stropping surface

## Natural Cordage

### Making Rope from Plant Fibers
Strong natural cordage can be made from: basswood inner bark, cedar bark, yucca leaves, stinging nettle stalks, milkweed stalks, dogbane, or any long fibrous plant material.

**Reverse-Wrap (Two-Ply) Technique:**
1. Harvest long fibers and let them dry partially (pliable but not brittle)
2. Take a bundle of fibers and fold at the center, creating two legs of roughly equal thickness
3. Pinch the fold point between thumb and finger
4. Twist the top leg away from you
5. Wrap the top leg over the bottom leg (toward you)
6. The former bottom leg is now on top — twist it away from you
7. Fold it over the other leg toward you
8. Continue this pattern: twist away, fold toward
9. Splice in new fibers by overlapping ends 3-4 inches before the current fibers run out
10. The more you twist each leg, the tighter and stronger the cord

### Paracord — The Universal Survival Cordage
Standard 550 paracord has a minimum breaking strength of 550 pounds and contains 7 inner strands inside a braided outer sheath.
- The outer sheath works as general cordage
- Inner strands can be extracted for: fishing line, sewing thread, dental floss, snare triggers, and fine lashing
- Always carry a minimum of 50 feet in your survival kit

## Improvised Tools

### Digging Stick
Sharpen a green hardwood stick and fire-harden the point by heating it over coals (not flames) and allowing it to cool. Rotate frequently to avoid burning. Use for: digging roots, creating fire pits, planting.

### Stone Cutting Tools
Select a flat stone with a naturally sharp edge. For basic knapping, strike the edge of a flint or obsidian nodule with a harder hammerstone at a sharp angle to detach flakes. The flakes make excellent cutting tools.

### Bone Needles
Splinter a long bone and grind one end to a point on a flat stone. Use a sharp stone flake to drill or scrape an eye hole near the blunt end.

### Containers from Bark
Birch bark can be folded into waterproof containers, sealed with pine pitch at the seams. Other barks can be folded into dry-storage containers for food and small tools.

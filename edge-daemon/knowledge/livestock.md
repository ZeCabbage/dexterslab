# Livestock Management

## Raising Animals for Off-Grid Self-Sufficiency

Livestock provides protein (eggs, milk, meat), fertilizer, pest control, and fiber. Start small with one species, learn its needs, then expand. Chickens are the best beginner animal for most homesteads.

## Chickens

### Starting a Flock
- Begin with 4-6 hens for a small family (expect 4-5 eggs per week per hen from good laying breeds)
- Best beginner breeds: Rhode Island Red (reliable layers, cold-hardy), Plymouth Rock (dual purpose — eggs and meat), Australorp (record-laying breed), Orpington (docile, cold-hardy)
- Hens lay eggs without a rooster. You only need a rooster for fertilized eggs (hatching new chicks)
- Pullets (young hens) start laying at 5-6 months old

### Housing
- Coop: 2-4 square feet per bird inside, more in cold climates where birds are confined longer
- Run: 8-10 square feet per bird in an outdoor enclosure
- Nesting boxes: 1 box per 3-4 hens, mounted 18 inches off the floor, filled with straw or wood shavings
- Roosting bars: 8-12 inches per bird, higher than the nesting boxes
- Predator proofing: use 1/2-inch hardware cloth (NOT chicken wire — predators tear through chicken wire). Bury mesh 12 inches underground or extend 18 inches outward to prevent digging predators
- Ventilation: openings near the roof for air circulation. Moisture buildup causes respiratory disease

### Feeding
- Quality layer feed (16-18% protein) as the primary diet
- Supplement with kitchen scraps, garden waste, and free-range foraging
- Calcium supplement: crushed oyster shell or dried, crushed eggshells in a separate dish
- Grit (small stones/coarse sand): necessary for digestion — chickens have no teeth and grind food in their gizzard
- Always provide fresh, clean water — chickens drink 1-2 cups per bird per day (more in heat)

### Common Issues
- Egg-eating: collect eggs frequently. Ensure adequate nesting box bedding and calcium
- Molting: annual feather replacement — egg production drops. Normal, not illness
- Predators: raccoons, foxes, hawks, dogs, snakes. Secure coop at night without fail

## Goats

### Why Goats
- Dairy goats produce 1-3 quarts of milk per day (Nigerian Dwarf to Nubian breed range)
- Goat milk is easily digestible and makes excellent cheese, yogurt, and soap
- Meat breeds (Boer, Kiko) provide high-quality protein
- Excellent brush clearers — they eat weeds, poison ivy, and invasive plants
- Goats are herd animals — always keep at least TWO

### Housing and Fencing
- Three-sided shelter minimum (they need dry shelter from rain but tolerate cold well)
- Bedding: straw or wood shaving, clean regularly to prevent hoof rot and parasites
- Fencing is critical — goats are legendary escape artists. 4-foot woven wire fence minimum. 5 feet recommended. Electric fence additions help. No gaps — they will find and exploit them
- Provide elevated surfaces (wooden platforms, large rocks) — goats love to climb and it supports hoof health

### Feeding
- Primary diet: quality grass hay (60-80% of diet). Alfalfa hay for milking does (higher protein and calcium)
- Goat-specific grain or pellets as supplement, especially for milking/growing animals
- Minerals: goat-specific loose mineral mix (NOT sheep minerals — goats need copper, which is toxic to sheep)
- Fresh clean water always available
- Browse: goats prefer brush, leaves, and bark over grass. Allow access to varied plants when possible

### Breeding
- Breeding season: typically fall (September-December) with kids born in spring
- Gestation: 150 days (approximately 5 months)
- Separate bucks from does except during planned breeding — bucks smell intensely and produce off-flavored milk if housed with milking does
- One buck can service 25-50 does

## Rabbits

### Why Rabbits for Survival
- Extremely efficient meat producers. One doe can produce 50-60 pounds of meat per year
- Quiet — no noise to attract attention
- Require very little space
- 30-day gestation, large litters (6-12 kits), and rapid maturity (ready for harvest at 8-12 weeks)
- Manure is "cold" — can be applied directly to garden without composting
- Can be raised on foraged greens, hay, and garden waste

### Housing Options
- **Hutches**: Wire-bottomed cages (1/2 × 1 inch wire) elevated on legs. Droppings fall through for easy collection. Minimum 6 square feet per adult rabbit. Protect from sun, rain, and wind
- **Colony**: Ground-level enclosure where rabbits live in a group. More natural behavior but harder to manage breeding and territorial aggression
- All housing must be secure from predators — especially dogs, cats, raccoons, hawks, and snakes

### Feeding
- Base diet: quality grass hay (timothy, orchard grass) — unlimited access
- Rabbit pellets (16-18% protein) as supplement
- Fresh greens daily: dandelion, clover, plantain, kale, carrot tops, herb trimmings
- Fresh water always available — rabbits dehydrate quickly
- Avoid: iceberg lettuce (too watery, no nutrition), potatoes, tomato leaves, rhubarb, avocado

### Breeding
- Bring the doe TO the buck's cage (never the reverse — does are territorial and may attack the buck)
- Return doe to her cage after breeding
- Provide a nesting box 3 days before her due date (day 28)
- Fill nesting box with hay — doe will pull fur to line the nest
- Check kits daily for the first few days — remove any that did not survive

## General Livestock Health

### Daily Observation Checklist
- Eating and drinking normally?
- Active and alert behavior?
- Clear eyes and nose (no discharge)?
- Normal droppings (consistent in size, color, and firmness)?
- Limping or favoring a leg?
- Visible wounds, swelling, or hair/feather loss?

### When Something Is Wrong
- Isolate sick animals immediately to prevent disease spread
- Monitor temperature if thermometer is available (normal variations: chickens 105-107°F, goats 101.5-103.5°F, rabbits 101-103°F)
- Ensure hydration — offer water with electrolytes (same ORS recipe as for humans in first-aid)
- Contact a veterinarian if available. If not, consult your reference materials for symptom-specific treatment
- Record what you observe — keeping health records helps identify patterns

## Butchering Basics

Humane dispatch is the ethical responsibility of anyone raising animals for food.
- Method must be quick and minimize suffering
- Bleeding: hang the animal head-down immediately after dispatch to drain blood. Thorough bleeding improves meat quality and shelf life
- Evisceration: remove internal organs carefully without puncturing the intestines
- Cool the carcass rapidly — hang in cool shade or immerse in cold water
- Process (cut into usable portions) as soon as the carcass has cooled
- Preserve by smoking, curing, canning, or drying if refrigeration is unavailable. See food-preservation

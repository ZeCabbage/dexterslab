# Food Farming and Agriculture

## Growing Food for Survival and Self-Sufficiency

Subsistence farming is the foundation of long-term survival. Focus on high-calorie crops, soil health, and seed saving to create a sustainable food supply independent of external inputs.

## Soil Building and Composting

Healthy soil is the foundation of all food production. Build soil before planting.

### Composting
Mix carbon-rich "browns" (dried leaves, straw, cardboard, wood chips) with nitrogen-rich "greens" (food scraps, fresh grass clippings, manure) at approximately a 30:1 carbon-to-nitrogen ratio.

**Hot composting method:**
1. Build pile at least 3 feet × 3 feet × 3 feet for sufficient heat
2. Layer browns and greens, moisten each layer
3. Turn the pile every 3-7 days to introduce oxygen
4. Internal temperature should reach 130-160°F to kill weed seeds and pathogens
5. Finished compost is dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling — typically ready in 1-3 months

### Other soil improvement methods
- **Cover crops**: Plant clover, rye, or vetch in fallow beds to fix nitrogen and prevent erosion. Cut and turn into soil before next planting
- **Mulching**: 3-4 inches of straw, leaves, or wood chips on soil surface suppresses weeds, retains moisture, feeds soil organisms
- **Worm bins**: Red wiggler worms convert kitchen scraps into nutrient-rich castings. Can operate indoors year-round

## High-Calorie Survival Crops

These crops produce the most calories per square foot of garden space:

**Potatoes**: Highest calorie yield per area. Plant seed potatoes 4 inches deep, 12 inches apart. Hill soil around stems as they grow. Harvest when foliage dies back. Store in cool (38-45°F), dark, humid conditions for months.

**Sweet Potatoes**: Heat-loving, drought-tolerant. Plant slips after last frost. Extremely productive in warm climates. Cure at 85°F for 10 days after harvest for best storage.

**Corn**: Calorie-dense staple. Plant in blocks (not rows) for wind pollination — minimum 4×4 block. Three Sisters method: plant with beans and squash.

**Dry Beans**: Protein and calorie-rich. Plant after last frost. Allow pods to dry completely on the plant. Store dried beans for years in airtight containers.

**Winter Squash**: Butternut, acorn, and Hubbard squash store for 3-6 months without processing. Plant in hills with plenty of space. Cure in sun for 10 days after harvest.

**Wheat/Grain**: If you have space, grain provides the foundation of bread and pasta. Requires larger growing area — approximately 1000 square feet per person per year for basic grain needs.

## Crop Rotation

Rotate crops each season to prevent disease buildup and maintain soil fertility:

**Year 1 — Legumes**: Beans, peas, clover. These fix nitrogen from the air into the soil.
**Year 2 — Heavy feeders**: Corn, squash, tomatoes, peppers. These use the nitrogen fixed by last year's legumes.
**Year 3 — Light feeders**: Root crops — carrots, beets, turnips, onions. Less demanding on soil nutrients.
**Year 4 — Rest/Cover crop**: Plant clover or rye. Let the soil recover.

## Companion Planting

Plants that benefit each other when grown together:

**Three Sisters (corn + beans + squash)**: Corn provides structure for beans to climb. Beans fix nitrogen for the corn. Squash leaves shade the ground, suppressing weeds and retaining moisture.

**Tomatoes + Basil**: Basil repels certain pests that attack tomatoes.
**Carrots + Onions**: Each repels the other's primary pest fly.
**Marigolds**: Plant borders of marigolds around gardens — they repel many common pests.

**Avoid planting together**: Tomatoes and brassicas (cabbage family). Beans and onion family. Fennel near most vegetables.

## Seed Saving

Seed saving ensures you never depend on external seed suppliers.

**Critical rule**: Save seeds only from open-pollinated or heirloom varieties. Hybrid seeds (marked F1) will not produce true-to-type plants in the next generation.

**Basic seed saving by crop:**
- **Tomatoes**: Squeeze seeds into water, let ferment 2-3 days (kills seed-borne diseases), rinse, dry completely on paper plate
- **Beans/Peas**: Leave pods on plant until fully dry and rattling. Shell and store
- **Squash/Cucumber**: Let fruit fully mature past eating stage. Scoop seeds, rinse, dry completely
- **Lettuce**: Let one plant bolt to flower. Collect seeds when fluffy heads form
- **Peppers**: Let fruit turn fully ripe (red). Scrape seeds, dry completely

**Storage**: Store dry seeds in paper envelopes inside airtight containers with silica gel packets. Keep cool and dark. Most seeds viable 2-5 years with proper storage.

**Isolation distances**: To prevent cross-pollination between varieties, maintain recommended distances: corn (1 mile), squash (1/2 mile), peppers (500 feet), beans/tomatoes (10-50 feet — mostly self-pollinating).

## Season Extension

Grow food beyond the normal growing season:

**Cold frames**: Bottomless box with transparent lid (old window, clear plastic). Place over beds to trap solar heat. Extends season 4-8 weeks in spring and fall.

**Row covers**: Lightweight fabric draped over hoops. Protects from light frost (down to 28°F) and insect pests while allowing rain and light through.

**Hoop houses**: Larger semi-permanent structures made from bent PVC or metal hoops covered in greenhouse plastic. Can enable year-round growing in most climates.

**Succession planting**: Plant new crops every 2-3 weeks throughout the growing season for continuous harvest rather than one large harvest.

## Irrigation Without Electricity

**Drip irrigation from rain barrels**: Gravity-fed systems work with as little as 2 feet of elevation. Use drip tape or soaker hoses that require minimal pressure.

**Swales**: On-contour berms that capture rainwater and allow it to slowly soak into the soil downhill. Reduces need for irrigation by capturing and storing natural rainfall.

**Hugelkultur beds**: Raised beds built over buried logs and branches. The decomposing wood acts as a sponge, storing water and releasing it slowly to plant roots. Reduces watering needs by 50-75% after the first year.

**Ollas**: Unglazed clay pots buried in the soil and filled with water. Water seeps slowly through the clay walls directly to plant roots. Ancient and highly efficient irrigation method.

## Pest Management Without Chemicals

- **Companion planting**: As described above
- **Crop rotation**: Breaks pest and disease cycles
- **Hand picking**: Physically remove large pests (caterpillars, beetles, slugs)
- **Beneficial insects**: Encourage ladybugs, lacewings, and parasitic wasps by planting flowers (dill, yarrow, fennel)
- **Physical barriers**: Row covers, copper tape for slugs, netting for birds
- **Diatomaceous earth**: Sprinkle around plants — kills soft-bodied insects by damaging their exoskeleton
- **Neem oil spray**: Natural insecticide effective against many common garden pests

# Medicinal Plants

## Using Plants for Basic Medical Needs

Plants have been used as medicine for thousands of years and contain real pharmacologically active compounds. However, misidentification can be fatal, dosing is imprecise, and plants alone cannot treat serious injuries or infections. Use this knowledge as a supplement to proper first aid, never as a replacement.

## Critical Safety Warnings

- NEVER eat or apply any plant you cannot positively identify with 100% confidence
- NEVER put foraged plant material into open wounds — you may introduce bacteria, parasites, or allergens
- Always test for allergic reaction: apply a small amount to unbroken skin and wait 30 minutes before broader use
- Pregnant women should avoid most medicinal plants
- These are traditional uses with varying levels of scientific evidence — they are not guaranteed treatments

## Pain and Fever Reduction

### Willow Bark (Salix species)
Willow bark contains salicin, which the body converts to salicylic acid — the same active compound family as aspirin.

**Preparation:** Strip the inner bark (the moist layer between the outer bark and the wood) from young branches. Chew the bark directly, or simmer 2 tablespoons of inner bark in 2 cups of water for 15 minutes to make tea.

**Dosage:** 1-2 cups of tea per day. Not a precise dose — start small.

**Precautions:** Same blood-thinning effects as aspirin. Do not use if you have bleeding disorders or take blood-thinning medication. Do not give to children (Reye's syndrome risk, same as aspirin). May cause stomach irritation.

### Meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria)
Also contains salicylates. Make tea from the flowers. Gentler on the stomach than willow bark.

## Respiratory Relief

### Pine Needle Tea
Rich in vitamin C (5 times the concentration of orange juice) and has decongestant properties.

**Preparation:** Gather fresh green needles from most pine species. Chop or bruise them. Steep a small handful in a cup of hot water for 10-15 minutes. Strain.

**Avoid:** Yew tree needles (Taxus — highly toxic), Norfolk Island pine, and Ponderosa pine (may be harmful to pregnant women).

### Mullein (Verbascum thapsicum)
The large fuzzy leaves make a soothing tea for respiratory congestion and cough.

**Preparation:** Dry the leaves. Steep 1-2 teaspoons of dried leaf in hot water for 10 minutes. Strain thoroughly through a fine cloth (the leaf hairs can irritate the throat if not filtered out).

## Digestive Aid

### Peppermint / Spearmint (Mentha species)
Widely recognized for relieving nausea, stomach upset, and digestive cramping.

**Preparation:** Fresh or dried leaves steeped in hot water for 10 minutes. Drink as tea.

### Ginger (Zingiber officinale)
Effective for nausea, motion sickness, and digestive discomfort. One of the most well-studied herbal remedies.

**Preparation:** Slice fresh ginger root thinly. Steep in hot water for 15-20 minutes. Stronger = more slices.

### Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla)
Calming, anti-inflammatory, mild sedative. Good for anxiety, stomach upset, and sleep support.

**Preparation:** Steep dried flowers in hot water for 5-10 minutes. Mild, pleasant taste.

## Wound Support (External Use Only)

### Plantain (Plantago major/lanceolata)
One of the most common and useful wild plants for minor wound care.

**Use:** Crush or chew fresh leaves into a poultice (a wad of mashed plant material). Apply to insect stings, minor cuts, blisters, and rashes. The plant contains aucubin and allantoin, which have anti-inflammatory and cell-regenerating properties.

### Yarrow (Achillea millefolium)
Traditionally used to slow bleeding from minor wounds. Its Latin name comes from the legend that Achilles used it to treat battlefield wounds.

**Use:** Crush fresh leaves and flowers. Apply directly to minor cuts and scrapes. Has mild antimicrobial and styptic (blood-stopping) properties.

**Note:** Some people are allergic to yarrow (ragweed family). Test on unbroken skin first.

### Raw Honey
If available, raw unprocessed honey has well-documented antimicrobial properties. Applied to minor wounds, it creates a moist healing environment while inhibiting bacterial growth. Used medically (Manuka honey) for wound care.

## Immune Support

### Elderberry (Sambucus nigra)
Has evidence supporting immune-boosting effects, particularly against viral upper respiratory infections.

**Critical warning:** Raw elderberries, bark, and leaves contain cyanogenic glycosides — they are MILDLY TOXIC when raw. Berries MUST be cooked before consumption. Simmer crushed berries in water for 30+ minutes. Strain and use as syrup.

### Garlic (Allium sativum)
Contains allicin, a compound with broad antimicrobial properties when consumed raw.

**Use:** Crush a clove and eat raw, or mince and add to food after cooking. Crushing activates the allicin. Cooking destroys most of the active compound.

## Preparation Methods

### Tea / Infusion
Pour hot (not boiling) water over leaves or flowers. Steep 10-15 minutes. Strain. Best for delicate plant parts.

### Decoction
For tougher plant parts (roots, bark, seeds). Simmer in water for 20-30 minutes. Strain. More concentrated extraction.

### Poultice
Crush or chew fresh plant material into a paste. Apply directly to the skin and cover with a clean cloth bandage. Replace every few hours.

### Tincture
Soak plant material in high-proof alcohol (vodka, grain alcohol) for 4-6 weeks in a sealed jar. Shake daily. Strain. The alcohol extracts and preserves the active compounds. Take small amounts by the dropperful.

### Salve
Infuse dried herbs in oil (olive oil, coconut oil) by warming gently for several hours. Strain out plant material. Mix infused oil with melted beeswax (approximately 1 ounce beeswax per 4 ounces oil) and pour into containers. Use for skin conditions, minor wounds, and muscle aches.

# Fire Craft

## How to Start a Fire in a Survival Situation

Fire provides warmth, water purification, cooking, signaling, light, psychological comfort, and predator deterrence. The fire triangle requires three elements: Heat (ignition source), Air (oxygen), and Fuel (dry material). Remove any one and the fire dies.

## Tinder — The First Catch

Tinder must be fine, dry, and fibrous to catch an initial spark or ember. Gather much more than you think you need.

**Natural tinder:**
- Birch bark — contains volatile oils, burns even when damp. Peel thin strips
- Cattail fluff — the seed heads ignite instantly from a spark
- Dried grasses — bundle loosely into a bird-nest shape
- Fatwood — resin-saturated pine heartwood found in dead pine stumps. Shave into fine curls
- Cedar bark — pull apart and shred into fine fibers
- Old Man's Beard lichen — found hanging from trees in damp forests
- Pine pitch/resin — natural accelerant, sticky and highly flammable
- Dried pine needles — good secondary tinder

**Prepared tinder (carry in your kit):**
- Cotton balls coated in petroleum jelly — the gold standard. Burns 3-5 minutes
- Dryer lint — catches sparks easily but burns fast
- Char cloth — charred cotton fabric, catches the faintest spark
- Wax-dipped cardboard — waterproof and long-burning
- Commercial fire starters — fire putty, WetFire cubes

## Kindling and Fuel

**Kindling** (pencil-thin to finger-thick sticks):
- Gather dead twigs still attached to trees — drier than ground material
- Process into uniform sizes for consistent burning
- Need at least two large handfuls before attempting ignition

**Fuel** (wrist-thick and larger):
- Dead standing wood is driest
- Split wet logs to access dry interior wood
- Hardwoods (oak, maple, hickory) burn hotter and longer
- Softwoods (pine, cedar, spruce) ignite easier but burn faster

## Ignition Methods

### Ferrocerium Rod (Ferro Rod)
The most reliable modern fire-starting tool. Produces sparks at approximately 3000°C. Works when wet.

**Technique:**
1. Prepare tinder bundle on a flat surface
2. Hold the rod close to the tinder (1-2 inches away)
3. Place striker or knife spine at 45-degree angle against the rod
4. Pull the rod backward while keeping the striker stationary — this prevents scattering tinder
5. Direct sparks into the center of the tinder bundle
6. Once tinder catches, gently blow at the base to feed oxygen

### Bow Drill (Friction Fire)
A primitive but effective method. Requires practice, proper materials, and dry wood.

**Components needed:**
- **Fireboard**: Flat piece of dry softwood (cottonwood, willow, cedar, poplar). Carve a small depression and cut a V-notch to the edge
- **Spindle/Drill**: Straight dry stick, 12-18 inches long, thumb-thick. Carve points on both ends
- **Bow**: Curved branch with cordage tied between ends (shoelace, paracord, or natural cordage works)
- **Bearing block/Handhold**: Hardwood, bone, or stone with a small depression to hold the top of the spindle. Lubricate with oil or wax to reduce friction at the top
- **Coal catcher**: Bark strip or leaf placed under the V-notch

**Technique:**
1. Wrap the bow string once around the spindle
2. Place spindle point into the fireboard depression
3. Press bearing block down on top of spindle
4. Use long, full strokes with the bow keeping consistent pressure
5. Smoke should appear. Increase speed and pressure
6. Hot black dust accumulates in the V-notch and forms a glowing ember
7. Carefully transfer ember to tinder bundle
8. Gently blow into tinder bundle until it bursts into flame

### Flint and Steel
Classic method using a hard striker against high-carbon steel.
- Strike downward with sharp edge of flint against the steel
- Direct sparks onto char cloth (pre-charred cotton fabric)
- Transfer glowing char cloth into tinder bundle
- Blow gently to flame

## Fire Lays — Structuring for Success

### Teepee Fire Lay
Best for getting fire started quickly. Place tinder bundle in center. Lean kindling sticks together forming a cone shape. Leave an opening on the windward side for lighting. As kindling catches, add larger sticks maintaining the cone shape.

### Log Cabin Fire Lay
Build a square structure of parallel sticks stacked in alternating directions like building a log cabin. Place tinder in the center. Provides stable structure and good airflow. Excellent for cooking — creates hot coal bed.

### Lean-To Fire Lay
Push a green stick into the ground at a 30-degree angle pointing into the wind. Lean kindling against the windward side. Place tinder underneath. The stick acts as a windbreak protecting the growing flame.

### Pyramid / Upside-Down Fire
Stack largest logs on the bottom in parallel. Layer progressively smaller pieces on top. Place tinder and kindling on the very top. Fire burns downward and is self-feeding. Requires almost no maintenance once lit.

## Starting Fire in Wet Conditions

Wet weather is the most challenging fire scenario. Extra preparation is essential.

1. **Find dry material:** Dead branches still attached to trees are drier than anything on the ground. Look for standing deadwood.
2. **Process fuel:** Split wet logs with a knife or hatchet to expose dry inner wood. Shave thin curls from the dry interior to create "feather sticks" — fine wood curls still attached to the stick that ignite easily.
3. **Elevate the fire:** Build on a platform of rocks or a layer of green logs to keep the fire above wet ground and melting snow.
4. **Create a shelter:** If raining, rig a tarp or bark shelter above the fire area before starting. Rain dripping into a new fire will kill it.
5. **Pre-dry fuel:** Once you have a small fire going, prop damp fuel nearby to let the heat drive out moisture. Do not place directly in the fire until surface-dried.
6. **Use accelerants if available:** Petroleum jelly, hand sanitizer, insect repellent, or pine resin all help sustain flame on damp tinder.

## Fire Safety

- Clear a 10-foot diameter area to bare soil or rock before starting
- Never leave fire unattended
- Keep water or dirt nearby to extinguish
- Extinguish by drowning with water, stirring ashes, and drowning again. Feel with the back of your hand — if warm, add more water.
- In windy conditions, dig a fire pit or build a windbreak
- Never build fire against a standing tree, under low branches, or near dry brush

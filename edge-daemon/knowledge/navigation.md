# Navigation Without Technology

## Finding Your Way Without GPS or Electronics

When electronic navigation fails, celestial observation, terrain reading, and dead reckoning become essential survival skills. Practice these methods in familiar territory before depending on them in emergencies.

## Determining Direction Using the Sun

The sun rises in the east and sets in the west. At solar noon (highest point), it is due south in the Northern Hemisphere and due north in the Southern Hemisphere.

### Shadow Stick Method
1. Place a straight stick (3 feet tall) vertically in flat, level ground
2. Mark the tip of its shadow with a stone
3. Wait 15-20 minutes
4. Mark the new position of the shadow tip
5. Draw a line between the two marks — this line runs approximately east-west
6. The first mark (longer shadow) is west, the second mark is east
7. Stand with west to your left and east to your right — you are facing north

### Analog Watch Method
In the Northern Hemisphere:
1. Hold the watch horizontal
2. Point the hour hand directly at the sun
3. The point halfway between the hour hand and the 12 o'clock position indicates south
4. If using daylight saving time, use the 1 o'clock position instead of 12

In the Southern Hemisphere: point 12 o'clock at the sun. The midpoint between 12 and the hour hand indicates north.

## Star Navigation — Northern Hemisphere

### Finding Polaris (North Star)
Polaris sits almost exactly above true north and barely moves as other stars rotate around it.

1. Locate the Big Dipper (Ursa Major) — seven bright stars forming a ladle shape
2. Find the two stars at the outer edge of the bowl (farthest from the handle) — these are the "pointer stars" Dubhe and Merak
3. Draw an imaginary line through these two pointer stars extending upward about 5 times the distance between them
4. This line points directly to Polaris
5. Polaris is the last star in the handle of the Little Dipper (Ursa Minor)

**If the Big Dipper is below the horizon:** Use Cassiopeia, a W-shaped constellation on the opposite side of Polaris from the Big Dipper. Polaris lies roughly between the Big Dipper and Cassiopeia.

### Using Any Star for Direction
If you cannot identify Polaris:
1. Stick two sticks in the ground several feet apart, aligned with any bright star
2. Watch the star's movement over 15-20 minutes relative to the sticks:
   - Star moves up: you are facing east
   - Star moves down: you are facing west
   - Star moves right: you are facing south
   - Star moves left: you are facing north

## Star Navigation — Southern Hemisphere

### Using the Southern Cross
1. Locate the Southern Cross (Crux) — four bright stars forming a cross shape
2. Extend the long axis of the cross about 4.5 times its length toward the horizon
3. This point marks the approximate South Celestial Pole
4. Drop a line straight down to the horizon from this point — that is due south

## Dead Reckoning

Dead reckoning estimates your current position based on a known starting point, direction of travel, and distance covered.

### Pace Counting
1. On flat ground, count your paces (a pace = two steps, left-right) over a measured 100 meters
2. Average person takes 62-68 paces per 100 meters on flat ground
3. Record your personal count — this is your "pace factor"
4. In rough terrain, your pace count increases (multiply flat-ground count by 1.5)
5. Going uphill adds approximately 1 pace per 5 degrees of slope

### Keeping a Dead Reckoning Log
Write down each "leg" of your journey:
- Direction of travel (compass bearing or natural landmark reference)
- Number of paces
- Estimated distance
- Time departed and time arrived
- Notable landmarks passed

This allows you to retrace your exact route if you need to return.

## Terrain Reading and Map Skills

### Topographic Map Basics
- **Contour lines** show elevation. Lines close together = steep slope. Lines far apart = gentle slope
- **Index contours** (thick lines) are labeled with elevation numbers
- Contour lines forming V-shapes pointing uphill indicate valleys or streams
- Contour lines forming V-shapes pointing downhill indicate ridges
- Closed circles = hilltops or depressions (check elevation labels to determine which)

### Terrain Association
Constantly compare what you see to your map:
- **Handrails**: Linear features (rivers, ridgelines, trails, power lines) that run parallel to your direction of travel. Follow them to stay on course
- **Catch features**: Obvious landmarks that tell you you have gone too far (a river crossing, a cliff, a major road)
- **Attack point**: An identifiable feature near your destination that you navigate to first, then make the final short approach from there

### Mental Mapping Without a Map
When you have no written map:
- Constantly observe and note prominent landmarks: mountain peaks, river bends, large trees, rock formations
- Note sun position relative to your direction of travel (if walking toward the morning sun, you are heading east)
- Track the direction of flowing water — streams flow to rivers, rivers flow to larger landmarks
- Listen for road noise, water sounds, or other indicators of civilization

## Compass Improvisation

### Magnetizing a Needle
1. Find a steel needle or straight pin
2. Stroke it in ONE direction (not back-and-forth) against a magnet, silk cloth, or even your hair 50-100 times
3. The needle is now weakly magnetized

### Making a Floating Compass
1. Place the magnetized needle on a small leaf or thin piece of cork
2. Float it on still water in a cup or puddle
3. The needle will slowly rotate to align north-south
4. Determine which end is north by checking against a known direction (sun position)

### Limitations
- Improvised compasses are affected by nearby metal objects
- They indicate magnetic north, which differs from true north by the local "declination" angle
- Accuracy is limited — use as a general directional guide, not for precise navigation

## The STOP Protocol — When You Are Lost

**S — Sit**: Stop moving immediately. Sit down. Panic causes people to walk in circles and waste energy.
**T — Think**: Recall your last known position. What landmarks did you pass? What direction were you heading?
**O — Observe**: Look around. Do you recognize anything? Can you see or hear roads, water, or civilization? Check the sun or stars for direction.
**P — Plan**: Decide your next action deliberately. Should you backtrack to your last known point? Stay put and signal for help? Follow a water source downhill?

**If truly lost with no hope of self-rescue:** Stay put, create shelter, make yourself visible (bright clothing, signal fire, ground-to-air signals). Searchers will cover your last known area first.

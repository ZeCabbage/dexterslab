# First Aid

## Emergency Medical Care in a Survival Situation

When professional medical help is unavailable, proper first aid saves lives. Focus on stabilizing the patient, preventing the condition from worsening, and promoting recovery.

## Patient Assessment — The ABCs

Always follow this sequence when approaching an injured person:

1. **Scene Safety**: Make sure the area is safe for you. You cannot help if you become a second casualty
2. **Airway**: Is the airway open? Tilt head back, lift chin. Clear any obstructions
3. **Breathing**: Is the patient breathing? Look for chest rise, listen for breath sounds, feel for air on your cheek
4. **Circulation**: Is there a pulse? Check for severe bleeding. Apply pressure to any life-threatening hemorrhage immediately
5. **Head-to-Toe Exam**: Systematically check for injuries from head to feet. Remove clothing if needed to expose wounds
6. **Monitor Vitals**: Check pulse rate, breathing rate, and level of consciousness regularly

## Bleeding Control

### Direct Pressure
For all wounds: apply firm, direct pressure with the cleanest available material. Maintain pressure for at least 10-15 minutes without peeking. If blood soaks through, add more material on top — do not remove the original dressing.

### Wound Packing
For deep wounds that cannot be controlled with surface pressure: pack the wound cavity firmly with clean gauze or cloth strips, pressing material deep into the wound. Apply direct pressure on top.

### Tourniquets
Last resort for life-threatening limb bleeding that cannot be controlled with direct pressure.
- Apply 2-3 inches above the wound (never on a joint)
- Use a wide band (2+ inches) — belt, torn fabric, or commercial tourniquet
- Tighten until bleeding stops completely
- Note the exact time of application — write "TK" and the time on patient's forehead or the tourniquet itself
- Do NOT remove a tourniquet once applied — this can cause fatal shock from released toxins

## Wound Care and Infection Prevention

### Cleaning Wounds
1. Wash your hands thoroughly or wear gloves
2. Irrigate the wound with the cleanest water available — use pressure (squeeze bottle or improvised syringe from a plastic bag with a small hole)
3. Remove all visible debris, dirt, and dead tissue
4. Flush from inside the wound outward

### Closing Wounds
- Use butterfly closures or adhesive strips (steri-strips) for clean, straight cuts
- Pull wound edges together — do not overlap
- Apply strips perpendicular to the wound line
- Do NOT close: animal bites, puncture wounds, heavily contaminated wounds, wounds older than 6-8 hours. These need to drain to prevent trapped infection

### Dressing and Monitoring
- Cover with sterile or clean dressing
- Change dressing daily and inspect the wound
- Signs of infection: increasing redness spreading from wound, warmth, swelling, pus or cloudy discharge, red streaking away from wound, fever, increased pain after initial decrease
- If infection develops: clean wound aggressively, apply antibiotic ointment if available, keep wound open to drain, increase fluid intake

## Splinting Fractures and Sprains

The goal of splinting is to immobilize the injury to prevent further damage and reduce pain.

**Rules:**
1. Splint in the position found — do not force a limb back to normal position
2. The splint must immobilize the joint above AND below the fracture
3. Pad the splint well — rigid materials should not press directly on skin or bony prominences
4. Use rigid materials: boards, straight branches, trekking poles, rolled magazines, SAM splints
5. Secure with strips of cloth, belts, or tape — firm but not so tight it cuts circulation
6. After splinting, check CSM (Circulation, Sensation, Movement) in fingers or toes every 15-30 minutes:
   - Circulation: are they warm? Is there a pulse below the splint? Press fingernail and it pinks up within 2 seconds?
   - Sensation: can the patient feel you touching their fingers/toes?
   - Movement: can the patient wiggle fingers/toes?
7. If CSM is compromised, loosen the splint immediately

## Burns Treatment

### First Actions
1. Remove the patient from the heat source
2. Cool the burn with cool (not cold or ice) running water for 10-20 minutes
3. Remove jewelry, watches, and clothing near the burn before swelling starts
4. Do NOT remove clothing stuck to the burn

### Burn Care
- Cover loosely with a clean, non-stick sterile dressing or clean plastic wrap
- Do NOT pop blisters — they protect the wound underneath
- Do NOT apply butter, oil, toothpaste, or any home remedy
- Pain management: ibuprofen or acetaminophen if available
- Keep the patient warm — burns compromise temperature regulation
- Elevate burned limbs above heart level to reduce swelling

### Seek Help For
- Burns covering more than 10% of body surface area
- Burns on face, hands, feet, groin, or over joints
- Full-thickness burns (white, brown, or charred appearance with no pain = nerve damage)
- Chemical or electrical burns
- Burns in patients under 5 or over 60 years old

## CPR — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation

When a person is unresponsive and not breathing normally:

1. Place on firm, flat surface
2. Position heel of one hand on center of chest (lower half of sternum)
3. Place other hand on top, interlace fingers
4. Push hard and fast: compress at least 2 inches deep
5. Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute (rhythm of "Stayin' Alive" by Bee Gees)
6. Ratio: 30 compressions then 2 rescue breaths
7. For rescue breaths: tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, create seal, breathe in for 1 second watching for chest rise
8. Continue without stopping until help arrives, the patient recovers, or you are physically unable to continue
9. If unwilling or unable to do rescue breaths, hands-only CPR (continuous compressions) is still effective

## Treating Shock

Shock is a life-threatening condition where the body is not getting enough blood flow. It can follow severe bleeding, burns, allergic reactions, or dehydration.

**Signs:** Pale, cool, clammy skin. Rapid weak pulse. Rapid shallow breathing. Confusion or anxiety. Nausea. Thirst.

**Treatment:**
1. Lay the patient flat on their back
2. Elevate legs 8-12 inches (unless spinal injury suspected)
3. Keep patient warm with blankets, coats, or sleeping bag — insulate from ground
4. Do NOT give food or drink (exception: if shock is from dehydration and patient is alert, give small sips of water)
5. Monitor breathing and pulse frequently
6. Treat the underlying cause (stop bleeding, treat burns, etc.)
7. Calm and reassure the patient — anxiety worsens shock

## Dehydration Treatment

**Signs in order of severity:**
- Mild: dark yellow urine, dry mouth, thirst, slight headache
- Moderate: very dark urine or decreased output, dizziness, fatigue, rapid heartbeat
- Severe: no urine output, confusion, fainting, rapid weak pulse, sunken eyes

**Treatment:**
- Oral rehydration solution: mix 1/2 teaspoon salt + 6 teaspoons sugar in 1 liter of clean water
- Give small frequent sips — not large gulps (may cause vomiting)
- If patient is vomiting, give 1 teaspoon every 1-2 minutes
- Continue until urine returns to pale yellow and normal volume
- Prevention: drink before you feel thirsty, especially in hot weather or during physical exertion

## Hypothermia

**Signs:** Shivering (stops in severe hypothermia — a dangerous sign), confusion, slurred speech, clumsiness, drowsiness.

**Treatment:**
1. Get patient out of cold, wind, and wet
2. Remove wet clothing, replace with dry layers
3. Insulate from the ground — this is critical
4. Warm the core first: warm (not hot) drinks if conscious, hot water bottles/heated rocks wrapped in cloth placed in armpits and groin
5. Do NOT rub extremities — this can push cold blood to the core and cause cardiac arrest
6. Handle gently — a severely hypothermic heart is very sensitive to rough movement
7. Share body heat in a sleeping bag if no other heating source available

## Heat Illness

### Heat Exhaustion
Signs: heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, headache, dizziness, muscle cramps.
Treatment: move to shade, rest, cool with wet cloths, drink water with electrolytes.

### Heat Stroke (Life-Threatening Emergency)
Signs: body temperature above 103°F, hot red dry skin (sweating stops), confusion, loss of consciousness.
Treatment: cool aggressively — immerse in cold water if possible, pack ice/cold cloths in armpits, neck, and groin. This is a medical emergency.

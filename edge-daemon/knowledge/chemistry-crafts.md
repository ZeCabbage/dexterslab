# Chemistry and Practical Crafts

## Essential Chemistry and Crafting Skills for Off-Grid Living

These practical skills allow you to produce essential goods — soap, charcoal, leather, candles — from raw materials available in nature or on the homestead.

## Soap Making

Soap is produced through saponification: a chemical reaction where fats or oils combine with a strong alkali (lye) to produce soap and glycerin.

### Making Lye from Wood Ash
1. Burn hardwood (oak, ash, maple — avoid softwoods) to clean white ash
2. Fill a barrel or bucket with ash. Prop it up with a hole in the bottom and a filter layer (straw or cloth)
3. Slowly pour rainwater through the ash. Collect the brown liquid that drains out — this is potassium hydroxide (lye water)
4. Test strength: a chicken feather dissolved in the lye water indicates sufficient strength. Or float a fresh egg — if it floats enough that a quarter-sized area is above the surface, the lye is ready
5. If too weak, pour the lye water through fresh ash again, or boil it down to concentrate

### Cold Process Soap
1. Melt your fat source: rendered animal fat (tallow or lard) or plant oils (olive oil, coconut oil)
2. Heat fat to approximately 100-110°F
3. Carefully add lye to water (NEVER water to lye — it can erupt violently). Stir until dissolved. Let cool to approximately 100°F
4. Slowly pour lye solution into the melted fat while stirring constantly
5. Continue stirring until mixture reaches "trace" — when it thickens to the consistency of thin pudding and drizzled soap leaves a visible trail on the surface
6. Pour into molds (wooden boxes lined with parchment, or any non-reactive container)
7. Cover and insulate for 24 hours
8. Remove from mold and cut into bars
9. Cure (air-dry) for 4-6 weeks before use — this allows the saponification reaction to complete fully

**Safety:** Lye causes severe chemical burns. Always wear gloves and eye protection. Work in a well-ventilated area. Keep vinegar nearby to neutralize skin contact. Keep away from children and animals.

## Charcoal Production

Charcoal is produced through pyrolysis — heating wood in an oxygen-starved environment to drive off moisture and volatile compounds, leaving nearly pure carbon.

### Retort Method (Tin Can Method)
1. Pack dry hardwood pieces tightly into a metal container (coffee can, paint can, or metal drum)
2. Punch several small holes in the lid for gases to escape
3. Place the sealed container IN a fire (the fire provides heat but the container restricts oxygen around the wood inside)
4. Gases escaping through the lid holes will initially be smoky (water vapor and volatiles). They will then become flammable (combustible gases). When the gases stop burning, the process is complete
5. Remove the container from the fire and seal the holes with mud or metal. Allow to cool completely before opening
6. Result: lightweight, black, porous charcoal

**Uses for charcoal:**
- Fuel: burns hotter and cleaner than wood, essential for blacksmithing
- Water filtration: crushed charcoal removes tastes, odors, and some chemicals from water (does not kill pathogens)
- Soil amendment: biochar mixed into garden soil improves water retention and nutrient availability
- Drawing and marking
- Medical: activated charcoal treats certain poisonings (consult medical guidance for dosing)

## Leather Tanning

Converting raw animal hides into durable, flexible leather for clothing, containers, straps, and accessories.

### Brain Tanning (Traditional Buckskin)
The most accessible survival method — uses the animal's own brain as the tanning agent.

1. **Fleshing**: Stretch the fresh hide over a log or beam. Scrape all flesh, fat, and membrane from the inside using a dull blade or bone scraper
2. **De-hairing**: Soak the hide in water with wood ash (lye solution) for 3-7 days until the hair slips easily. Scrape off all hair and the outer grain layer
3. **Rinsing**: Rinse thoroughly in clean water to remove all lye
4. **Brain solution**: Mash the animal's brain (every animal has enough brain to tan its own hide) in warm water until dissolved in a smooth slurry
5. **Application**: Work the brain solution thoroughly into both sides of the damp hide. Let it soak in for several hours to overnight. Repeat the application 2-3 times
6. **Softening**: This is the most labor-intensive step. As the hide dries, stretch, pull, and work it continuously over a breaking edge (a smooth, rounded surface like a smooth branch or rope). Every fiber must be broken while drying. If you stop before it is fully dry, the hide will become stiff
7. **Smoking**: Sew the hide into a bag shape and suspend over a small smoky fire (punk wood, not flames) for 15-30 minutes per side. The smoke deposits oils that make the leather water-resistant. Without smoking, brain-tanned leather re-stiffens when wet

### Vegetable Tanning
Uses tannins from tree bark (oak, hemlock, chestnut) to permanently alter the hide protein. Produces stiffer, more durable leather ideal for belts, sheaths, and tooling. Takes weeks to months but produces a very different product than brain tanning.

## Candle Making

### Tallow Candles (from Animal Fat)
1. Render fat: cut animal fat into small pieces. Heat slowly in a pot over low heat until the fat melts and separates from connective tissue (cracklings)
2. Strain through cheesecloth into a clean container. Let solidify
3. Re-melt the rendered tallow. You can purify by melting in water, letting it solidify, and scraping off the clean tallow layer from the top
4. Create wicks: twist cotton string or use braided cotton cord. Dip wick in melted tallow and let it harden straight
5. Dip method: Tie wick to a stick. Dip into melted tallow. Let cool and harden. Repeat dipping until candle reaches desired thickness (20-30 dips)
6. Mold method: Pour melted tallow into a mold (tin can, carved wood, rolled paper tube) with the wick suspended in the center

### Beeswax Candles
Beeswax burns longer, brighter, and cleaner than tallow with a pleasant honey scent. If you keep bees, harvest wax from old comb. Melt, strain, and use the same dipping or molding process as tallow.

## Blacksmithing Basics

### Improvised Forge
1. Build a contained fire pit from firebricks, stone, or a thick metal container
2. Air source: attach a pipe leading to the bottom of the fire pit connected to a bellows (two boards hinged at one end with a leather or cloth piece creating a pumping chamber) or a hand-cranked blower
3. Fuel: hardwood charcoal (not briquettes — those add chemicals). Coal if available
4. Anvil: any heavy, flat, smooth piece of steel or iron (railroad track, large hammer head, thick steel plate)

### Basic Forging Operations
1. **Drawing out**: Lengthening metal by hammering on the anvil face. Hit at a slight angle for best metal flow
2. **Upsetting**: Shortening and thickening metal by hammering on the end
3. **Bending**: Heating to orange-red and bending over the anvil edge or in a vise

### Heat Treatment
- **Quenching**: Heating steel to cherry red and rapidly cooling in oil or water. This hardens the steel
- **Tempering**: After quenching, reheat gently to a specific temperature (indicated by oxide colors on the surface — straw yellow for blades, light blue for springs) then let air cool. This reduces brittleness while maintaining hardness

## Natural Dyes

### The Process
1. Mordant the fabric first: simmer in water with alum (aluminum potassium sulfate — 10% of fabric weight) for 1 hour. This helps dye bind permanently
2. Prepare dye bath: simmer the color source in water for 30-60 minutes. Strain out plant material
3. Immerse pre-mordanted fabric in the dye bath. Simmer gently for 1 hour, stirring occasionally
4. Remove, rinse in cool water, hang to dry

### Color Sources
- Yellow/orange: onion skins, turmeric, marigold flowers
- Brown: walnut hulls, oak bark, tea, coffee
- Red/pink: madder root, beets, avocado pits and skins
- Blue: indigo leaves (requires special processing), blueberries (may fade)
- Green: combine blue + yellow dyes, or use nettle leaves
- Black: walnut hulls + iron mordant, oak galls + iron

# Food Preservation

## Preserving Food Without Refrigeration

Food preservation converts seasonal abundance into year-round sustenance. These methods predate electricity and remain essential for off-grid survival. Master multiple techniques to ensure dietary diversity through all seasons.

## Drying and Dehydration

Removes moisture to prevent bacterial, yeast, and mold growth. Dried foods are lightweight, compact, and store for months to years.

### Sun Drying
- Requires hot (85°F+), dry, breezy conditions
- Slice food thin (1/8 to 1/4 inch) for fastest drying
- Place on clean screens or racks elevated off the ground
- Cover with cheesecloth to protect from insects
- Bring indoors at night to avoid moisture reabsorption
- Takes 2-4 days depending on conditions

### Oven or Fire Drying
- Set oven to lowest setting (130-170°F) with door cracked for air circulation
- Over a fire: build a drying rack 3-4 feet above low coals (not flames)
- Slice food thin and spread in single layer
- Check every 30 minutes, rotate for even drying
- Done when leathery with no moisture when squeezed

### What to Dry
- **Fruits**: Apples, berries, grapes (raisins), bananas. Pre-treat with lemon juice to prevent browning
- **Vegetables**: Tomatoes, peppers, onions, corn, beans, peas
- **Herbs**: Bundle and hang upside down in warm, dry, ventilated area
- **Meat (jerky)**: Cut lean meat into thin strips with the grain. Marinate in salt and spices. Dry at 145-165°F until it bends and cracks but does not break

## Smoking

Smoking preserves through dehydration, antimicrobial compounds in smoke, and surface protection from a glaze of deposited chemicals.

### Cold Smoking (below 90°F)
- Primarily for flavor and long-term preservation
- Meat must be cured with salt FIRST
- Smoke continuously for 12-48 hours at 68-86°F
- Results in shelf-stable product when properly cured and smoked

### Hot Smoking (126-176°F)
- Cooks and preserves simultaneously
- Takes 4-8 hours depending on item thickness
- Results in ready-to-eat product but with shorter shelf life than cold smoking
- Fish, poultry, and sausages are common candidates

### Building a Simple Smokehouse
1. Any enclosed chamber that can trap smoke works — barrel, wooden box, or stone/brick enclosure
2. Fire or coals go in a separate firebox connected by a pipe or trench to the smoke chamber (for cold smoking)
3. For hot smoking, coals can be inside the chamber
4. Use only hardwood — apple, hickory, oak, cherry, maple. NEVER use pine, cedar, or other conifers (toxic resin)
5. Control airflow with adjustable vents at top — more ventilation = less smoke density

## Salt Curing

Salt draws moisture from food through osmosis, creating an environment hostile to bacteria.

### Dry Curing
1. Coat meat generously with salt — approximately 1 pound of salt per 4 pounds of meat
2. Pack salted meat in a container, cover completely with salt
3. Store in cool location (36-40°F ideal)
4. Small pieces cure in 1-2 weeks. Large hams take 1-2 months
5. After curing, soak in fresh water for several hours to reduce saltiness before cooking

### Brine Curing
1. Dissolve salt in water until an egg floats (approximately 1 pound salt per 1 gallon water)
2. Submerge meat completely in brine
3. Weight it down to keep submerged
4. Store in cool location
5. Cure time: 3-5 days per pound of meat

## Fermentation

Lacto-fermentation uses beneficial bacteria to create an acidic environment that preserves food and enhances nutrition.

### Sauerkraut
1. Shred cabbage finely
2. Mix with salt — 2-3 tablespoons salt per 5 pounds of cabbage
3. Pack firmly into a clean jar or crock, pressing down until liquid covers the cabbage
4. Weight down to keep cabbage submerged below the brine
5. Cover with cloth to keep dust out while allowing gases to escape
6. Ferment at room temperature (60-75°F) for 1-4 weeks
7. Taste periodically — when sour to your preference, move to cold storage
8. Properly fermented sauerkraut keeps for months in cool conditions

### Basic Vegetable Fermentation
Almost any vegetable can be fermented using a 2-3% salt brine:
1. Dissolve 1-2 tablespoons of salt per quart of water (non-iodized salt — iodine inhibits fermentation)
2. Pack vegetables into a jar
3. Pour brine over vegetables until fully submerged
4. Keep submerged with a weight
5. Ferment 3-7 days at room temperature
6. Move to cold storage when desired sourness is reached

## Canning

### Water Bath Canning (High-Acid Foods Only)
Safe for: fruits, jams, jellies, pickles, tomatoes with added acid, fruit juices.

1. Sterilize jars and lids in boiling water
2. Fill jars with prepared food leaving appropriate headspace (typically 1/4 to 1/2 inch)
3. Remove air bubbles by sliding a non-metallic tool along the inside edge
4. Wipe jar rims clean and apply lids and bands finger-tight
5. Process in boiling water bath for the time specified by the recipe (typically 10-30 minutes depending on food and jar size)
6. Remove and let cool undisturbed for 12-24 hours
7. Check seals — lid should not flex when pressed

### Pressure Canning (Low-Acid Foods)
Required for: vegetables, meat, poultry, fish, soups, stews.

Low-acid foods can harbor Clostridium botulinum — the bacterium that causes botulism. Only pressure canning reaches temperatures high enough (240°F) to destroy these spores.

1. Follow tested recipes EXACTLY — this is a food safety issue, not a creativity exercise
2. Process at the pressure and time specified for your altitude
3. Do not open the canner until pressure has returned to zero naturally

**Critical warning:** NEVER water-bath can low-acid foods. NEVER modify pressure canning recipes. ALWAYS follow USDA or university extension tested recipes. Botulism is odorless, tasteless, and potentially fatal.

## Root Cellaring

Stores fresh produce for months using cool temperatures, darkness, and controlled humidity.

**Ideal conditions:** 32-40°F, 80-95% humidity, dark, with some ventilation.

**What stores well:**
- Potatoes: 3-6 months (do not store near apples — ethylene causes sprouting)
- Carrots: 4-6 months (pack in damp sand or sawdust)
- Beets: 3-5 months
- Turnips: 3-4 months
- Onions: 4-8 months (need drier conditions — 60-70% humidity)
- Apples: 2-6 months depending on variety (store separately — they release ethylene gas)
- Cabbage: 3-4 months (hang upside down by root)
- Winter squash: 3-6 months (cure first at 80°F for 10 days)

**DIY root cellar options:**
- Insulated corner of unheated basement
- Buried chest freezer (turned off) with ventilation
- Well-insulated outdoor pit lined with straw
- Buried 55-gallon plastic drum with drainage

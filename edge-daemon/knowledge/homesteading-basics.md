# Homesteading Basics

## Planning and Managing an Off-Grid Homestead

A successful homestead integrates shelter, water, food production, energy, and community into a self-reinforcing system. Start small, learn from each season, and expand deliberately.

## Site Assessment

Before committing to a location, evaluate these factors:

### Sun Exposure
- Food gardens need minimum 6 hours of direct sunlight daily
- South-facing slopes (Northern Hemisphere) receive the most sun
- Map shade patterns throughout the day — note where trees, buildings, or terrain block light
- Solar panels also need unobstructed sun — plan their placement alongside garden areas

### Water Access
- Identify all water sources: streams, springs, ponds, groundwater depth, annual rainfall
- Test water quality before relying on natural sources
- Plan rainwater collection capacity based on roof area and average precipitation
- Consider irrigation distance from water source to garden areas

### Soil Quality
- Do a jar test: fill a mason jar 1/3 with soil, add water, shake vigorously, let settle for 24 hours. Sand settles first (bottom), then silt (middle), then clay (top). Ideal garden soil: roughly 40% sand, 40% silt, 20% clay
- Test pH with a simple kit: most vegetables prefer pH 6.0-7.0. Amend with lime (raises pH) or sulfur (lowers pH)
- Good soil drains well but retains moisture. Dig a hole 12 inches deep, fill with water. If it drains within 1-4 hours, drainage is good

### Wind and Climate
- Note prevailing wind direction — plant windbreaks (rows of trees) on the windward side
- Track frost dates: last spring frost and first fall frost determine your growing season length
- Microclimate identification: south-facing walls radiate heat (good for tender plants), low spots collect cold air (frost pockets to avoid)

## Permaculture Zoning

Organize your homestead by frequency of human interaction:

**Zone 0 — The Home**: The house itself. Focus on energy efficiency, thermal comfort, natural lighting, indoor food sprouting and herb growing

**Zone 1 — Daily Attention** (immediate surroundings):
- Kitchen garden (herbs, salad greens, frequently harvested vegetables)
- Compost bins
- Chicken coop
- Rainwater collection tanks
- Workshop and tool storage
- Handwashing and sanitation facilities

**Zone 2 — Regular Attention** (short walk):
- Larger vegetable garden (main food production)
- Fruit trees and berry bushes (orchard)
- Bee hives
- Goat or rabbit shelters
- Greenhouse or hoop house
- Root cellar

**Zone 3 — Occasional Attention** (further out):
- Field crops (wheat, corn, potatoes at scale)
- Managed pasture for larger livestock
- Hay production
- Managed woodlot for firewood and building materials

**Zone 4 — Minimal Management**:
- Wild harvest areas (nuts, berries, medicinal plants)
- Managed forest (selective logging, mushroom cultivation)
- Large water features (ponds for fish, irrigation)

**Zone 5 — Observation Only**:
- Undisturbed wilderness
- Wildlife habitat
- Study and observation area

## First-Year Priority Checklist

1. **Secure water supply**: Working well, spring, or rainwater collection system with reliable filtration
2. **Garden establishment**: Start with raised beds or clear a small garden area. Plant immediately — even a late garden produces something
3. **Composting**: Begin composting on day one. Everything organic goes in. You can never have too much compost
4. **Firewood**: A cord of wood (4×4×8 feet stacked) heats an efficient home for about one month. Calculate your winter needs early
5. **Chicken coop**: Simple, predator-proof, positioned in Zone 1. Source hens locally
6. **Food preservation**: Root cellar (even a simple insulated box in the ground), drying racks, canning supplies
7. **Tool organization**: Secure, dry storage for all tools. Maintain edges on cutting tools. Inventory what you have and what you need
8. **Perimeter security**: Basic fencing, entrance management, and early-warning measures

## Seasonal Planning

### Spring
- Start seeds indoors 6-8 weeks before last frost
- Prepare garden beds: amend soil with compost, till or broadfork
- Plant cold-hardy crops first: peas, lettuce, spinach, radishes, onions
- Breed livestock for spring births
- Repair winter damage to structures and fences
- Collect and store rainwater during spring rains

### Summer
- Main growing season: plant warm-season crops after frost danger passes
- Harvest and preserve continuously — don't let anything go to waste
- Maintain irrigation, weeding, pest management
- Build and repair structures (good weather construction season)
- Harvest honey if keeping bees
- Dry herbs, make hay, prepare for winter early

### Fall
- Harvest remaining crops before first frost
- Intensive preservation: canning, drying, root cellaring, fermenting
- Plant garlic and cover crops for next year
- Process and store firewood
- Winterize water systems (drain exposed pipes, insulate tanks)
- Butcher livestock planned for winter meat supply
- Clean and store tools properly

### Winter
- Repair and maintain tools and equipment
- Plan next year: review what worked and what didn't. Order seeds
- Education: study new skills, practice knots, read reference materials
- Small indoor projects: candle making, soap making, leather working, basket weaving
- Monitor stored food: rotate stock, check for spoilage
- Care for overwintering livestock — ensure water doesn't freeze, provide extra feed

## Record Keeping

Maintain a homestead journal documenting:
- Weather observations (daily highs/lows, precipitation, frost dates)
- Planting dates and varieties used
- Harvest yields (helps plan quantities for next year)
- Livestock breeding records and health notes
- Equipment maintenance schedule
- Supply inventory (what you have, what you need, what runs out fast)
- Skills learned and mistakes made

This record becomes increasingly valuable each year — it is a personalized reference guide specific to your land, climate, and situation.

## Community and Trade

No homestead is truly self-sufficient in isolation. Build relationships with neighbors and community through:
- **Skill sharing**: Teach what you know, learn what you don't
- **Work parties**: Help with large projects (barn raising, harvest, fencing)
- **Trade networks**: Exchange surplus produce, eggs, milk, skills, and labor
- **Communication plans**: Establish how you will contact neighbors in emergency (radio, signal systems, designated check-in schedule)
- **Mutual aid agreements**: Formally agree on shared resource plans, emergency protocols, and defense cooperation

# Shelter — Permanent Construction

## Building Permanent Off-Grid Structures

When wilderness survival transitions to long-term off-grid living, permanent shelter construction becomes essential. These methods use locally sourced, natural building materials to create durable, weather-resistant, and thermally efficient homes without relying on modern supply chains.

## Foundation Principles

Every permanent structure needs a dry, stable foundation. Moisture is the primary enemy of natural building materials.

### Rubble Trench Foundation
The most commonly used foundation for natural building:
1. Dig a trench 2 feet wide and 1-2 feet below the frost line
2. Lay perforated drain pipe at the bottom, sloped to drain water away from the building
3. Fill the trench with compacted gravel (not round river rock — angular gravel interlocks better)
4. Level the top of the gravel
5. Build stem walls 12-18 inches above finished grade to protect walls from splashing rain and ground moisture

## Earthbag Construction (Superadobe)

Fill long polypropylene bags or individual sandbags with subsoil, tamp solid, and stack in courses to create extremely strong walls.

**Materials needed:** Polypropylene bags (feed bags work), subsoil (mix of sand, clay, and gravel), barbed wire, tamper

**Construction process:**
1. Fill bags with moistened subsoil mixed with 5-10% Portland cement for added strength (optional for load-bearing walls)
2. Lay bags in courses on the foundation, tamping each bag flat and solid after placement
3. Run 2 strands of 4-point barbed wire between each course — this acts as mortar, preventing bags from shifting
4. Stagger bag joints (like bricks) for structural integrity
5. Door and window frames are built as you go, with bags wrapping around them
6. Can be built as straight walls or curved into domes (domes are inherently structural and need no separate roof)
7. Plaster with earthen plaster or stucco for weather protection and aesthetics

**Advantages:** Extremely strong (earthquake, flood, bullet, fire-resistant), uses earth that is available everywhere, low skill requirement, minimal tools needed.

## Cob Construction

Cob is a mixture of clay-rich soil, sand, and straw mixed together and built up by hand in layers.

**Mixing ratio:** Approximately 1 part clay soil + 2 parts sharp sand + straw mixed in until the cob holds together without cracking and does not stick excessively to hands.

**Construction:**
1. Mix ingredients by foot-stomping on a tarp (the traditional method)
2. Form cob into balls or loaves
3. Place on the wall and blend into the previous course by pressing and kneading
4. Build walls in lifts of 12-18 inches per day maximum (allow each lift to firm up before adding the next)
5. Total wall thickness: 12-24 inches for load-bearing walls
6. Windows and doors are formed as you build by placing frames and building the cob around them
7. Cover fresh cob with tarps if rain threatens before it has dried

**Thermal properties:** Excellent thermal mass — absorbs heat during the day and radiates it at night. Best for climates with day/night temperature swings. Less effective as pure insulation in consistently cold climates.

## Straw Bale Construction

Straw bales provide exceptional insulation (R-value of 25-35 for a standard bale wall).

**Two approaches:**
- **Load-bearing:** The bales themselves carry the weight of the roof. Simpler but limited to single-story structures
- **Post-and-beam (infill):** A timber frame carries the structural load. Bales are stacked as insulating infill

**Critical rules for straw bale building:**
1. Use only DRY bales (moisture content below 20%). Wet bales rot
2. Protect bales from moisture: wide roof overhangs (24 inches minimum), high stem walls (18+ inches off ground), breathable plaster (not cement stucco directly on straw — use lime or clay plaster)
3. Stack bales flat (strings horizontal) in a running bond pattern (stagger like bricks)
4. Pin bales together with rebar or bamboo stakes driven vertically through each course
5. Plaster both sides with lime or clay plaster for fire protection, weatherproofing, and structural stiffening

## Log Cabin Construction

The traditional method using stacked, interlocked logs.

### Log Selection and Preparation
- Select straight, consistent-diameter logs 8-12 inches in diameter minimum
- Debark fresh logs to prevent insect damage
- Let logs season (dry) for 6-12 months if possible. Green logs shrink as they dry, opening gaps
- Sort logs by size — use the largest for bottom courses

### Notching Methods
- **Saddle notch**: A U-shaped scoop in the bottom of each log that seats over the log below. Simple and effective
- **Dovetail notch**: An interlocking notch that tightens as logs settle. Most weathertight but hardest to cut
- **Butt and pass**: Alternating logs that extend past the corner. Simplest but least weathertight

### Chinking
Fill gaps between logs with a combination of:
- Inner layer: oakum, moss, or fiberglass insulation
- Outer layer: mortar mix, clay/lime plaster, or commercial chinking compound
- Re-chink annually until logs fully settle (typically 2-3 years)

## Essential Knots for Construction

See tools-knots for full knot details. Most important for building:
1. **Bowline** — fixed loop for anchoring ridgepoles and lifting materials
2. **Trucker's hitch** — mechanical advantage for tensioning roof lines
3. **Square lashing** — joining poles at right angles for scaffolding and frames
4. **Clove hitch** — quick attachment of ropes to poles during construction
5. **Timber hitch** — dragging and lifting logs

## Roofing Options for Natural Buildings

### Living Roof (Green Roof)
Heavy but well-insulated. Build a strong roof frame, cover with plywood or boards, layer: waterproof membrane → drainage layer (gravel) → soil → plants. Weight: 20-35 lbs per square foot — structure must handle this.

### Thatch
Traditional and effective where tall grasses or reeds are available. Properly laid thatch can last 20-40 years. Layer bundles of dry grass or reeds starting from the eaves and overlapping upward. Minimum 12-inch thickness. Steeper pitch = better water shedding.

### Metal Roofing
If available, the most durable and low-maintenance option. Corrugated metal roofing is lightweight, sheds water and snow effectively, and can last 50+ years. Channel into gutters for rainwater collection.

# Shelter — Wilderness Survival

## Building Emergency Shelter in the Wild

Shelter is your number one survival priority in harsh weather. The rule of threes: you can survive 3 hours without shelter in extreme conditions (but only 3 days without water and 3 weeks without food). Ground insulation is more important than roof insulation because the ground steals body heat 25 times faster than air.

## Site Selection

Choose your shelter site carefully before building.

**Good site characteristics:**
- Protected from prevailing wind (lee side of hills, rock outcroppings, dense tree stands)
- Elevated slightly above surrounding terrain (avoid cold air pooling in valleys and low spots)
- Near water source but above flood lines and away from dry river beds
- Near building materials (deadfall, leaves, branches)
- Flat or gently sloped ground for sleeping comfort
- Sun exposure (south-facing in Northern Hemisphere for morning warming)

**Avoid:**
- Hilltops and ridges (exposed to wind and lightning)
- Valley bottoms (cold air sinks and pools here, also flood risk)
- Under dead standing trees or large dead branches (widow-makers)
- Dry river beds or washes (flash flood risk even without local rain)
- Near animal trails or dens
- Near stagnant water (mosquitoes and insects)

## Debris Hut

The most effective emergency shelter for solo survival. Insulates extremely well and can be built with no tools.

**Construction:**
1. Find or create a ridgepole 9-12 feet long and sturdy enough to bear weight without flexing
2. Prop one end on a stump, rock, or low tree fork about 2-3 feet high. The ground end rests on the ground
3. Lean branches (ribs) along both sides of the ridgepole at 45-degree angles, spaced 8-12 inches apart
4. Fill in between ribs with smaller sticks to create a lattice framework
5. Pile debris (leaves, pine needles, grass, ferns) on top starting from the bottom and working up — like shingles on a roof. Build layer 2-3 feet thick minimum
6. Add a final layer of branches on top to hold debris in place against wind
7. Fill the inside completely with leaves for ground insulation and body insulation — you should burrow into the debris like a sleeping bag
8. Create a small door plug of stuffed debris to block the entrance

**Critical dimensions:** The shelter should be just wide enough for your body (arms at sides) and just tall enough to sit up at the high end. Smaller = warmer. You are heating the air inside with body heat alone.

## Lean-To Shelter

Simple and quick to build. Best for mild conditions with wind from one direction.

1. Find or lash a horizontal ridgepole between two trees at waist to chest height
2. Lean branches at 45-60 degrees against the ridgepole on the windward side
3. Layer evergreen boughs, bark strips, or large leaves over the frame starting from the bottom, overlapping like shingles
4. Build a reflector fire 4-6 feet in front of the open side to radiate heat into the shelter
5. Insulate the ground inside with 4-6 inches of dry leaves, boughs, or grass

**Limitations:** Open on one side — only blocks wind and rain from one direction. Less warm than an enclosed shelter.

## A-Frame Shelter

A lean-to with both sides covered. Better insulation and weather protection.

1. Set up a ridgepole between two supports (same as lean-to)
2. Lean branches against both sides of the ridgepole
3. Cover both sides with debris — thick layers
4. Leave one end open for entry (typically the downwind end)
5. The open end should face away from prevailing wind
6. Insulate ground heavily

## Snow Shelters

### Quinzhee (Mounded Snow Shelter)
Best when snow is not deep enough for a snow cave.

1. Pile loose snow into a mound 5-7 feet tall and 8-10 feet in diameter
2. Let the mound settle and sinter (snow crystals bond together) for at least 2 hours — longer is better
3. Insert sticks 12-18 inches into the mound all around as thickness guides
4. Hollow out the inside from one side, stopping when you reach the guide sticks
5. Make the ceiling dome-shaped (strongest against collapse)
6. Poke a ventilation hole near the top with a stick — critical for breathing
7. The sleeping platform should be higher than the entrance (warm air rises)
8. Block entrance with a pack or snow block, leaving ventilation gap

### Snow Cave
For deep snowpack on hillsides.

1. Find a deep, stable snowdrift on the lee side of a hill — avoid avalanche-prone slopes
2. Dig entrance tunnel slightly upward into the hillside
3. Excavate a sleeping chamber higher than the entrance tunnel (cold air sinks out, warm air stays)
4. Smooth the ceiling to prevent dripping (smooth surface channels melt water to walls)
5. Ventilation hole in ceiling is absolutely critical — carbon dioxide buildup kills silently
6. Interior temperature will stabilize around 32°F regardless of outside temperature

### Snow Trench (Emergency)
Quickest snow shelter — when time is critical.

1. Dig a body-length trench in deep snow
2. Cover with branches, a tarp, or snow blocks
3. Line bottom with insulation (pine boughs, anything between you and the snow)
4. Not as warm as a quinzhee but can be built in 30 minutes

## Tarp Shelter Configurations

With 50+ feet of paracord and a standard tarp, you can create several effective shelters:

**A-Frame tarp**: Tie ridgeline between two trees. Drape tarp over it. Stake out both sides. Simple and effective rain protection.

**Lean-to tarp**: Tie ridgeline at waist height. Pull one edge to ground and stake. Creates an angled roof with one open side. Build fire in front of open side.

**Diamond fly**: Hang tarp by one corner at head height. Stake opposite corner to ground. Stake remaining two corners out to the sides. Creates a low, wind-shedding shelter.

## Ground Insulation

**This is the most commonly overlooked survival shelter mistake.** The ground will steal your body heat far faster than cold air.

Minimum 4-6 inches of insulation between your body and the ground:
- Dry leaves (most commonly available — pile them 8+ inches as they compress)
- Evergreen boughs (excellent — arrange in overlapping rows like fish scales)
- Dry grass (good but compresses quickly)
- Bark strips (durable but less comfortable)
- Foam pad or emergency blanket (if available — mylar side facing up reflects body heat)

## Emergency Shelter Materials to Carry

- Emergency space blanket (mylar) — weighs 2 ounces, reflects 90% of body heat
- Contractor trash bag (55 gallon, 3 mil) — cut open as an emergency ground cloth or poncho shelter
- 50 feet of paracord — minimum for tarp shelters and lashing
- Lightweight tarp (8×10 minimum) — the most versatile shelter tool you can carry

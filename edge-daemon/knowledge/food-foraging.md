# Food Foraging

## Identifying and Harvesting Wild Edible Plants

Foraging supplements your food supply with free, nutrient-dense wild foods. However, misidentification can be fatal. Never eat a plant you cannot positively identify. When in doubt, do not eat it.

## The Universal Edibility Test

Use ONLY as a last resort when no other food is available. This does NOT guarantee safety — some toxins are not detected by this test. Test only ONE plant part at a time (leaf, root, stem, fruit — each may have different properties).

**Step-by-step procedure:**
1. Fast for 8 hours (drink only purified water)
2. Separate the plant into its parts (leaf, stem, root, flower, fruit)
3. Test for contact irritation: rub a small piece on the inside of your wrist or elbow. Wait 15 minutes. If no itching, burning, swelling, or rash — proceed
4. Lip test: touch a small piece to your outer lip. Wait 3 minutes. If no reaction — proceed
5. Tongue test: place a small piece on your tongue. Hold for 15 minutes. Do NOT chew or swallow. If numbness, stinging, burning, or extreme bitterness — spit out immediately and discard
6. Chew test: chew a small piece. Hold in mouth 15 minutes without swallowing. If no adverse reaction — proceed
7. Swallow test: swallow the small piece. Wait 8 full hours monitoring for nausea, cramps, or any adverse symptoms
8. If no reaction after 8 hours: eat a larger portion (1/4 cup). Wait another 8 hours
9. If still no reaction, the plant part is likely safe to eat in reasonable quantities

## Warning Signs — Do NOT Eat Plants With These Characteristics
- Milky or discolored sap (exceptions: dandelion, coconut)
- Bitter, soapy, or burning taste
- Beans, bulbs, or seeds inside pods
- Fine thorns or spines on stems and leaves
- Almond scent in leaves or bark (may indicate cyanide compounds)
- Grain heads with pink, purple, or black spurs (ergot fungus)
- Three-leaf growth pattern (poison ivy, poison oak, poison sumac)
- Shiny leaves with no identifiable plant characteristics

## Commonly Available Edible Wild Plants

### Dandelion (Taraxacum) — All Parts Edible
- Leaves: eat raw in salads or cook like spinach. Best before flowering (less bitter). Rich in vitamins A, C, K
- Roots: roast and grind as coffee substitute. Boil as a root vegetable
- Flowers: eat raw, batter and fry, or make tea
- Found: lawns, fields, roadsides throughout temperate regions worldwide

### Cattail (Typha) — The Supermarket of the Swamp
- Young shoots: peel outer leaves, eat inner core raw or cooked (tastes like cucumber)
- Immature flower heads: boil and eat like corn on the cob
- Pollen: shake yellow pollen from mature heads. Mix with flour for protein-rich bread
- Roots/rhizomes: peel, dry, and grind into flour. Or eat roasted
- Found: edges of ponds, marshes, ditches throughout North America

### Pine Trees — Multiple Uses
- Needles: steep in hot water for pine needle tea, rich in vitamin C (5 times more than oranges). Use from most pine species — avoid yew and Norfolk Island pine
- Inner bark (cambium): scrape the soft white layer between outer bark and wood. Eat raw or dry and grind into flour. Emergency calorie source
- Pine nuts: from pinecones of specific species (pinyon pine, stone pine)

### Clover (Trifolium) — Flowers and Leaves
- Flowers: eat raw or dried for tea. Good source of protein
- Leaves: eat young leaves raw. Older leaves are better cooked (can cause bloating if eaten raw in large quantities)
- Found: fields, lawns, roadsides worldwide

### Plantain (Plantago) — Not the Banana Relative
- Leaves: young leaves eaten raw or cooked. Older leaves are tough but edible when boiled
- Medicinal: crushed fresh leaves applied to insect stings, minor wounds, and rashes
- Found: lawns, trails, disturbed ground worldwide. One of the most common weeds

### Acorns — Calorie-Dense Nut
- Must be processed to remove bitter tannins before eating
- Shell acorns and grind or chop the nut meat
- Leach tannins by soaking in multiple changes of water (pour off and replace when water turns brown) until water runs clear and taste is no longer bitter. This may take many water changes
- Once leached, dry and grind into flour, or roast and eat as nuts
- White oak acorns have fewer tannins than red oak (less leaching needed)

## Insect Foraging

Insects are abundant, protein-rich, and calorie-dense. Most insects are safe to eat when cooked.

**Safe to eat (always cook first):**
- Grasshoppers and crickets: remove legs (barbed and can lodge in throat), wings, and antennae. Roast on hot rocks or skewer over fire
- Termites: high protein and fat content. Roast or eat after removing wings
- Grubs and beetle larvae: found in rotting logs. High fat content. Roast until crispy
- Ants: edible but small yield. Avoid fire ants. Some species taste lemony (formic acid)
- Earthworms: purge by keeping alive in damp grass for 24 hours (expels soil from gut). Then cook

**AVOID:**
- Brightly colored insects (red, orange, yellow) — warning coloration often indicates toxins
- Hairy or fuzzy caterpillars — may cause severe allergic reactions
- Insects that emit strong odors when disturbed
- Spiders, centipedes, scorpions, ticks
- Stinging insects (bees, wasps) unless desperate — remove stinger first
- Any insect found feeding on carrion or waste

## Mushrooms — Extreme Caution Required

Many deadly mushrooms closely resemble edible species. Even experienced foragers make fatal mistakes. Unless you have expert-level identification skills confirmed through extensive training, DO NOT eat wild mushrooms. The death cap (Amanita phalloides) kills several people annually and has no reliable distinguishing features for beginners.

## Seasonal Foraging Calendar

**Spring:** Young greens (dandelion, plantain, clover, wild garlic), shoots (cattail, fiddlehead ferns — cook fiddleheads thoroughly)
**Summer:** Berries (blackberry, blueberry, raspberry), flowers (clover, dandelion), seeds, young cattail heads
**Fall:** Nuts (acorns, walnuts, hickory), late berries, seeds, roots (cattail, burdock, chicory)
**Winter:** Inner bark, dried stores, roots from under snow, pine needle tea for vitamin C

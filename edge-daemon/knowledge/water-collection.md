# Water Collection and Storage

## Harvesting and Storing Water Off-Grid

Reliable water access is the foundation of any off-grid or survival situation. Rainwater harvesting, manual wells, and natural water collection provide independence from municipal water systems.

## Rainwater Harvesting

### Calculating Collection Potential
For every 1 inch of rain falling on 1,000 square feet of roof surface, you can collect approximately 600 gallons of water. A modest 1,500 square foot roof in an area receiving 30 inches of annual rainfall can collect 27,000 gallons per year.

### System Components
1. **Catchment surface**: Your roof. Metal roofs are best (cleanest runoff). Avoid asphalt shingle roofs for drinking water (chemical leaching). Cedar shake and tile are acceptable
2. **Gutters and downspouts**: Standard aluminum or vinyl gutters channel water from roof to storage. Install leaf guards or first-flush diverters to keep debris out
3. **First-flush diverter**: A simple device that diverts the first 10-20 gallons of each rain event (the dirtiest water) away from your storage tank. Can be built from PVC pipe with a ball valve
4. **Storage tanks**: Food-grade plastic tanks (IBC totes — 275 gallons each are affordable and common), polyethylene cisterns, or metal tanks. Keep opaque/dark to prevent algae growth
5. **Overflow**: Always include an overflow pipe directing excess water away from your foundation

### Gravity-Fed Irrigation
For every 1 foot of vertical elevation difference between your water tank and your irrigation point, you gain approximately 0.43 PSI of pressure.
- Elevate tanks on sturdy platforms (cinder blocks, reinforced wood) — a full 275-gallon IBC tote weighs over 2,200 pounds
- Use drip irrigation tubing or soaker hoses (low pressure requirement)
- Install a 100-micron inline filter between tank and irrigation lines to prevent clogging
- Keep all irrigation lines below the water level in the tank — gravity cannot push water uphill

## Manual Wells

### Driven Well (Well Point)
Best for sandy or loose soil with a shallow water table (under 25 feet).
1. A steel well point (perforated, pointed steel tube) is driven into the ground using a post driver or sledgehammer
2. Sections of steel pipe are threaded and added as the point goes deeper
3. When the water table is reached, water fills the pipe
4. A pitcher pump or suction pump is attached to the top
5. Prime the pump and begin drawing water

### Hand-Augered Well
For deeper wells or harder soils.
1. Use a bucket auger on extension rods to manually drill into the ground
2. Remove soil in small batches as you deepen the hole
3. Line the hole with PVC casing pipe as you go (prevents collapse)
4. Install a well screen (slotted pipe) at the water-bearing layer
5. Fill the annular space around the casing with pea gravel at the screen level

### Hand Pump Selection
- **Pitcher/suction pumps**: Work for wells up to 25 feet deep. Use atmospheric pressure to draw water up. Simple, cheap, easy to repair
- **Deep-well hand pumps**: Use a rod-and-cylinder mechanism that pushes water up from depth. Work for wells 25-200+ feet deep. More expensive and complex but essential for deep water tables
- **Rope pumps**: DIY option using a continuous loop of rope with rubber pistons passing through a PVC pipe. Simple to build and maintain. Works to moderate depths

### Well Safety
- Locate wells at least 50-100 feet from septic systems, latrines, livestock areas, and chemical storage
- Position on higher ground relative to potential contamination sources
- Seal the top of the well casing to prevent surface water infiltration
- Test water quality before drinking — at minimum treat with bleach or boiling until quality is confirmed
- Sanitize new wells with bleach solution (1/2 gallon of bleach per 100 gallons of well water volume), let stand 24 hours, then flush until chlorine smell dissipates

## Natural Water Collection

### Dew Collection
In areas with regular morning dew:
1. Spread a clean tarp or sheet of plastic on the ground or over vegetation before nightfall
2. In the morning, collect pooled dew by tilting the surface into a container
3. Alternatively, tie absorbent cloths around your ankles and walk through dewy grass at dawn. Wring water into a container
4. Yield: typically 1-2 cups per collection

### Solar Still
For extracting water from moist soil or contaminated water:
1. Dig a cone-shaped hole 3 feet across and 2 feet deep in the sunniest available location
2. Place a clean collection container at the bottom center
3. If available, add green vegetation or pour contaminated water into the hole around the container
4. Cover the entire hole with clear plastic sheeting
5. Seal the edges with soil or rocks to create an airtight seal
6. Place a small stone in the center of the plastic directly above the container to create a low point where condensation will drip
7. Solar heat evaporates moisture from the soil and vegetation. It condenses on the underside of the plastic (which is cooler) and drips into the container
8. Yield: typically 1-3 cups per day. Not a primary water source but can supplement

### Transpiration Bag
1. Tie a clear plastic bag around a leafy branch on a living tree or large bush
2. Seal the opening tightly around the branch
3. Position so the bag hangs down with a collection point at the lowest corner
4. Plants transpire water through their leaves — this condenses inside the bag
5. Yield: 1-4 cups per bag per day depending on plant type, size, and sun exposure
6. Use multiple bags on different branches for more water

## Long-Term Water Storage

### Treatment for Storage
- For long-term storage of treated water: add 1/4 teaspoon of unscented household bleach per gallon
- Use food-grade containers only
- Store in a cool, dark location (UV light degrades plastic and promotes algae)
- Label all containers with the fill date
- Rotate stored water every 6-12 months (re-treat with bleach when refilling)
- Avoid storing directly on concrete floors — place on pallets or plywood (concrete can leach chemicals and allows faster temperature changes)

### Emergency Water Reserves
The minimum recommended emergency water supply is 1 gallon per person per day. For a household of four:
- 72-hour emergency supply: 12 gallons minimum
- 2-week supply: 56 gallons
- 1-month supply: 120 gallons
- These numbers cover drinking and basic sanitation only — cooking and hygiene require additional water

### Greywater Recycling
Water from sinks, showers, and laundry (NOT from toilets) can be reused for non-food irrigation.
- Filter out food particles and debris
- Apply directly to soil around trees and non-edible plants
- Do not store greywater — use within 24 hours before bacteria multiply
- Do not use greywater on root vegetables or leafy greens eaten raw
- Avoid using greywater containing bleach, strong detergents, or chemical cleaners on plants

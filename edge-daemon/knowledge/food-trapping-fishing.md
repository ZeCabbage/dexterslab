# Food — Trapping and Fishing

## Passive Hunting and Fishing for Survival

Trapping and fishing are more energy-efficient than active hunting. Set multiple traps or lines, then focus on other survival tasks while they work. Success depends on placement — location is 90% of trapping.

## Trapping Principles

- Set many traps (10+ if possible) to increase odds of catching game
- Place traps on active animal trails — look for tracks, droppings, worn paths, chew marks
- Check traps at least twice daily — both to harvest catches and to prevent suffering
- Natural funnels are ideal locations: gaps under fallen trees, narrow paths between rocks, along fences or walls
- Minimize your scent — rub hands with charcoal, mud, or evergreen needles before handling traps

## Simple Snare

The most basic and effective survival trap for small game.

**Materials:** Wire (picture hanging wire or snare wire is best), strong cordage, or paracord inner strands

**Construction:**
1. Form a small loop at one end of the wire by twisting back on itself
2. Pass the other end through the small loop to create a sliding noose
3. For rabbits: make the noose approximately fist-width in diameter (3-4 inches)
4. Set the bottom of the noose about 3-4 inches (one hand-width) above the ground
5. Anchor the snare firmly to an immovable object (tree, deep-driven stake, or heavy log)
6. Suspend the noose in the animal's path using small twigs or natural supports

**Spring pole snare:** Bend a sapling over and attach the snare line to it via a trigger mechanism. When the animal enters the snare, the trigger releases and the sapling snaps upright, lifting the animal off the ground. This prevents escape and keeps the catch away from ground predators.

## Deadfall Trap

Uses a heavy rock or log to crush small game. Several trigger designs exist.

### Figure-4 Deadfall
1. Carve three sticks: vertical post, diagonal support, and horizontal bait stick
2. Notch the sticks so they lock together in a figure-4 shape
3. The trigger assembly holds a heavy flat rock (10-20 pounds for rabbit-sized game)
4. Bait the end of the horizontal stick
5. When the animal disturbs the bait stick, the trigger collapses and the rock falls

**Placement:** Near burrows, on known trails, or where you have seen animal activity. Place natural guide fences (rows of sticks) on either side to funnel the animal toward the trigger.

## Survival Fishing

Fishing is often the most accessible protein source — most waterways contain fish.

### Improvised Hooks
- Thorns (hawthorn, honey locust) — naturally sharp and curved
- Safety pins — open and bend to hook shape
- Wire — bend into J-shape with barb
- Carved bone — whittle a gorge hook (straight double-pointed piece that toggles sideways in the fish's throat)
- Soda can tabs — sharp edges work in a pinch

### Improvised Line
- Paracord inner strands (2-4 lb test)
- Dental floss
- Unraveled thread from clothing
- Natural plant fiber cordage

### Bait
- Worms (dig in moist soil near streams)
- Grubs (found under bark of dead trees)
- Insects (grasshoppers, crickets, flies)
- Small pieces of meat, cheese, or bread
- Berries and corn kernels for some species
- Shiny improvised lures (bent aluminum, colored thread)

### Setlines and Trotlines
Passive fishing — set and leave while you do other tasks.
1. Tie a main line across a stream between two fixed points or along the bank
2. Attach 5-10 shorter drop lines (12-18 inches) spaced 3-4 feet apart along the main line
3. Bait each hook
4. Check every few hours
5. Legal in most survival situations but regulated in normal times — check local laws

### Fish Weirs
Permanent or semi-permanent fish traps built in shallow water.
1. Build a V-shaped or heart-shaped wall of rocks in a shallow stream
2. The narrow end of the V points downstream
3. Water flows through but fish swimming upstream are funneled into the narrow gap
4. Build a holding pen at the narrow end to trap fish
5. Check regularly and harvest by hand or with a net

### Spearfishing
For clear, shallow water.
- Carve a spear point on a straight green stick 6-8 feet long, or lash a knife to the end
- For better odds, carve a multi-pronged spear (trident) from a split branch
- Important: aim BELOW the fish — light refraction makes fish appear higher in the water than they actually are. The deeper the water, the more you must aim below

## Game Processing

### Field Dressing (Gutting)
For all game, process as soon as possible to prevent spoilage:
1. Place animal on its back
2. Make a shallow cut from the breastbone to the pelvis — cut only through skin and muscle, NOT into the intestines
3. Do NOT puncture the intestines or bladder — their contents contaminate the meat
4. Reach inside and carefully pull out all internal organs
5. Save the heart, liver, and kidneys if in good condition — these are highly nutritious
6. Drain blood by hanging the carcass head-down
7. Rinse the body cavity with clean water if available
8. Cool the carcass as quickly as possible — hang in shade with good air circulation

### Skinning
1. Cut around the legs at the knee/hock joints
2. Cut along the inside of each leg to the centerline cut
3. Peel the skin away from the meat, using the knife to separate connective tissue where needed
4. The skin can be preserved for leather (see chemistry-crafts) or used immediately for cordage

### Cooking Wild Game
- Always cook wild game thoroughly — internal temperature of 165°F minimum
- Boiling extracts the most nutrition (drink the broth)
- Roasting over coals on a spit or green-wood rack
- Smoking for preservation (see food-preservation)
- Never eat the brain, eyes, or spinal cord of any wild game (prion disease risk in deer family)
